[EDITOR'S DRAFT] Proposed Verifiable Claims Architecture

self-sovereign - an architectural principle where the holder of a verifiable claim is in complete control of their identifier and where their verifiable claims are stored.

There is currently no widely used self-sovereign, privacy-enhancing standard for expressing and transacting verifiable claims (aka: credentials, attestations) via the Web. This a summary of a proposed architecture to address part of this problem.

Proposed Verifiable Claims Architecture Goals

  1. Create a standard way for users to assert their verifiable qualifications to a service provider in a way that reduces fraud and enhances website usability by removing the need to manually enter verifiable claims.
  2. Ensure that holders and their verifiable claims may be independent from service providers.
  3. Ensure that there is an interoperable standard capable of expressing verifiable claims that works in the same way across different market verticals.
  4. Re-use existing attribute-exchange protocols or create new protocols that are capable of transmitting the data in a privacy-enhancing way.

Architecture Block Diagram

Architecture Terminology

Verifiable Claim
A claim that can be cryptographically proven to be non-repudiable, authentic, and unaltered by bad actors.
Issuers
Provide verifiable claims to people and organizations.
Repositories
Store and curate verifiable claims on behalf of people and organizations.
Inspectors
Request verifiable claims from people and organizations in order to give them access to protected resources.
Holders
Receive verifiable claims from issuers, store them at repositories that they trust, and provide them to inspectors in order to get access to protected resources.
Decentralized Identifier Registry
Decentralized Identifiers are the mechanism by which users are able to control their identifiers. They store self-sovereign identifier documents for the purpose of claiming self-sovereign identifiers, updating the basic management information associated with a self-sovereign identifier, and verifying the cryptographic authenticity of verifiable claims associated with a self-sovereign identifiers.

Architecture Benefits to Stakeholders

Issuers
  • Levels competitive playing field (not just a few super-providers)
  • Ability to participate in broader ecosystem resulting in common tooling to issue verifiable claims
  • Avoidance of vendor-specific solutions and lock-in
  • Potential for reduced infrastructure needs due to self-sovereign architecture
Repositories
  • Level competitive playing field (not just a few super-providers)
  • Ability to participate in a broader ecosystem of common tooling to store verifiable claims
  • Higher-stakes verifiable claims being stored resulting in more value-added services
Inspectors
  • Ability to participate in a broader ecosystem of common tooling to inspect verifiable claims
  • Richer set of verifiable claims to choose from, resulting in better understanding of the user
  • Increased ability and choice to trust authenticity of verifiable claims
Holders
  • No identity provider lock-in
  • Digital claims that can be used in more than one location
  • Ability to aggregate verifiable claims as cohesive digital identities
  • Privacy-enhanced sharing mechanism
  • Control of confidential information
  • Elimination of repetitive input at websites
  • Reduction in the need to input personally identifiable information (PII)
